## Abstract
This study applies the Hamiltonian method to analyze the nonlinear magnetic topology induced by Resonant Magnetic Perturbations (RMPs) in tokamaks. We investigate the system’s chaotic behavior by comparing three methods: the renormalization method, Lyapunov exponents, and weighted Birkhoff average. A strong consistency is found among these methods in predicting the large-scale stochasticity threshold. The magnetic island width threshold provides a quantitative criterion for optimizing RMP-based edge-localized modes control, bridging a critical gap in plasma control strategies.

## Key Contributions
* **Establish a quantitative threshold model for Hamiltonian renormalization**: Extend the renormalization group method to the tokamak toroidal configuration, and derive a general Hamiltonian criterion that links the measurable magnetic island width to the onset condition of large-scale stochasticity.
* **Realize cross-validation of multiple chaos diagnosis methods**: Systematically compare three independent diagnostic techniques, namely the renormalization method, Lyapunov Exponent (LE) and Weighted Birkhoff Average (WBA). It verifies their high consistency in predicting the breaking threshold of KAM tori, and greatly improves the reliability of chaos onset judgment.
* **Provide experimentally verifiable benchmarks for RMP-based ELM control**: Numerical simulations based on the DIII-D edge pedestal configuration show that the theoretically predicted critical magnetic island widths (approximately 1.7 cm for RMP and 0.58 cm for MHD) are in high agreement with the latest experimental observations of ASDEX Upgrade, offering direct quantitative guidance for optimizing the parameters of resonant magnetic perturbation coils.
* **Reveal a new mechanism for stochasticity triggered by edge MHD self-coupling**: It is found that the mutual coupling between edge non-resonant MHD magnetic islands can trigger large-scale stochastic layers in advance at a lower threshold than the "RMP-edge MHD" interaction, providing a crucial physical perspective for understanding the evolution of transport barriers in the pedestal region.
